What Is Body Mass Index (BMI)?

Body Mass Index, commonly known as BMI, is a numerical value derived from a person's weight and height. It was first introduced by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the 1830s as a way to define the "average man" in statistical terms. Today, BMI is used worldwide by healthcare professionals, insurance companies, and public health organizations as a quick screening tool to categorize individuals into weight-status categories.

While BMI is not a direct measure of body fat, it correlates reasonably well with more accurate body fat measurements for the majority of the population. It is widely used because it is simple, inexpensive, and non-invasive â€” all you need is a scale and a measuring tape.

Step-by-Step: How to Calculate Your BMI

  1. Measure your weight in kilograms (or pounds)
  2. Measure your height in meters (or inches). Note: if measuring in centimeters, divide by 100 to convert to meters
  3. Square your height (multiply it by itself)
  4. Divide your weight by your squared height
  5. The result is your BMI

Limitations of BMI

While BMI is a useful screening tool, it has several well-documented limitations that are important to understand:

Muscle vs. Fat: BMI does not distinguish between muscle mass and fat mass. Athletes and individuals with high muscle mass may have a BMI in the "overweight" or even "obese" range despite having low body fat. For example, a bodybuilder weighing 100 kg at 1.80 m would have a BMI of 30.9 (classified as obese) despite potentially having a body fat percentage below 15%.

Age and Gender: The standard BMI categories do not account for differences in body composition related to age and gender. Women naturally carry more body fat than men, and body composition changes as we age, with older adults tending to have more fat and less muscle. Despite these variations, the same BMI thresholds are applied to all adults.

Ethnic Variations: Research has shown that BMI thresholds may not be equally applicable across all ethnicities. Asian populations, for instance, tend to have higher health risks at lower BMI levels. The WHO has suggested lower BMI cut-offs for Asian populations: overweight at 23.0 and obese at 27.5.

Fat Distribution: BMI does not indicate where fat is stored in the body. Visceral fat (fat around internal organs) is more dangerous than subcutaneous fat (fat under the skin). Two individuals with identical BMIs may have very different health risk profiles depending on their fat distribution patterns. Waist circumference and waist-to-hip ratio are better indicators of visceral fat.

Better Alternatives to BMI

For a more comprehensive health assessment, consider supplementing BMI with:

  • Waist circumference: Risk increases at >94 cm for men and >80 cm for women
  • Body fat percentage: Measured via DEXA scan, bioelectrical impedance, or skinfold calipers
  • Waist-to-hip ratio: Helps assess fat distribution
  • Blood work: Cholesterol, blood sugar, and inflammatory markers provide objective health data

Conclusion

BMI remains a valuable starting point for assessing weight status due to its simplicity and accessibility. However, it should never be the sole indicator used to make health decisions. If you have concerns about your weight or health, consult a healthcare professional who can evaluate your individual circumstances using multiple metrics.
